I first met Elizabeth Schuyler while passing through Albany during the war. She belonged to a world of stability, wealth, and family legacy — things I had never known. Though our meeting was brief and formal, her intelligence and warmth stood out to me, and I carried the memory of her long after I returned to military life.

1779

As the conflict stretched on, thoughts of Eliza resurfaced. When we began exchanging letters, I found comfort in her words amid the uncertainty of war. Our correspondence allowed me to reveal parts of myself I rarely showed — my ambitions, doubts, and hopes for a future beyond survival.

During the army’s winter encampment near Morristown, we met again in person. This time, our connection deepened quickly. Conversations that began politely soon turned meaningful, and I realized how deeply her presence grounded me in a life otherwise defined by urgency and conflict.

I feared that my lack of wealth and noble lineage would stand in the way of our relationship. Yet Eliza’s family welcomed me warmly, valuing my dedication to the Revolutionary cause and recognizing my potential. Their approval made the possibility of marriage feel real rather than distant.

1780

On a cold December day in Albany, Eliza and I were married at her family home. For someone who had grown up without permanence or security, the ceremony symbolized far more than romance — it marked the beginning of a family and a sense of belonging I had spent my life striving to earn.
