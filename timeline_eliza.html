I first met Alexander Hamilton when he passed through Albany during the war. He was different from the other officers I encountered — intense, articulate, and driven by a restless ambition. Though our interaction was short, I sensed a depth beneath his polished words.

As the war continued, Alexander and I began writing to one another. Through his letters, I came to understand both his brilliance and his vulnerability. Despite our differing backgrounds, his honesty and determination drew me closer, even from afar.

When we reunited during the winter encampment near Morristown, the familiarity from our letters made our connection immediate. Our conversations felt natural and sincere, and I began to imagine a future that extended beyond the expectations placed upon me by society.

Though I initially worried my family might object to Alexander’s lack of fortune, they were impressed by his character and ideals. Their acceptance affirmed my own feelings and gave me the confidence to choose love over convention.

We were married at my family’s home in Albany, surrounded by warmth despite the winter cold. In choosing Alexander, I chose a life shaped by shared purpose, resilience, and devotion — a decision rooted not in comfort, but in genuine affection and belief in who we could become together.
